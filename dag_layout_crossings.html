Can a DAG always be drawn without crossings?

A quick, honest answer to “it’s a DAG, so zero crossings must be possible” — and “force-directed on a DAG never crosses.” Every number below is computed live in your browser by the same algorithms, counting real segment intersections. Nothing is hand-drawn.

Verdict: No, not in general. Zero crossings is achievable only for planar DAGs. A non-planar DAG (a DAG whose underlying graph contains a K₅ or K₃,₃) provably cannot be drawn without crossings by any method — force-directed included — because planarity is a property of the graph, not of the algorithm. Your notebook’s DAG is planar, so there we can (and now do) reach 0.

2 · A non-planar DAG: K₃,₃ oriented top→bottom (0 is impossible)

Three sources a, b, c each point to three sinks x, y, z — all 9 edges. It’s acyclic (a genuine DAG) and it’s K₃,₃, the textbook non-planar graph. Its crossing number is 1: the best any drawing can do is a single crossing, and 0 is mathematically unreachable. Re-run the force sim as many times as you like — it never hits 0.

Force-directed doesn’t even find the minimum (1) — it settles into a higher-energy tangle.
Why no algorithm can win here
  • Acyclic: every edge goes top-row → bottom-row, so there’s no cycle. It is a DAG.
  • Non-planar: the underlying graph is K₃,₃, which by Kuratowski’s theorem cannot be embedded in the plane without an edge crossing.
  • Therefore: ≥ 1 crossing in every drawing. Force-directed, layered, spectral, hand-tuned — all bounded below by the graph’s crossing number, which is 1. (K₅ oriented i→j is the same story; our layered layout draws it with exactly 1 crossing, the optimum, but still not 0.)
  • And even when 0 is possible (planar case), finding a minimum-crossing layered order is NP-hard, so in general we minimize with a heuristic rather than guarantee optimum.
